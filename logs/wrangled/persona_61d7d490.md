# Persona 61d7d490: Grant Kowalski

## Profile
- **Persona ID:** 61d7d490
- **Name:** Grant Kowalski
- **Age:** 45-54
- **Profession:** Entrepreneur
- **Culture:** North American
- **Core Values:** Security
- **Bio:** Grant runs a small HVAC contracting business in suburban Minneapolis that he started twelve years ago after leaving a regional manager position at a national chain. He keeps a spreadsheet tracking monthly overhead down to the cent and has never missed a payroll, even during the 2020 slowdown when he deferred his own salary for four months. His wife Karen works as a dental hygienist and periodically suggests he could make more money going back to corporate, a conversation that reliably ends with Grant changing the subject.

## Entry 0 - 2025-05-26

Locked in the Trane contract for the Edina office park this morning. Six units, maintenance included, eighteen-month term. The margins are thin but it's recurring revenue and that's what matters right now. Sat in the truck after the handshake and just felt the weight come off for a second. Months of quoting and following up and it's done.

**Nudge:** "What was the weight about?"

**Response:** Payroll mostly. We added a third tech in February and every month since then I've been watching the numbers tighter than usual. This contract covers his salary through next winter. That's the weight—knowing I can keep the lights on for the guys who depend on me.

## Entry 1 - 2025-05-26

Got home from the Edina signing and Karen had dinner going already. Pork chops, which she only makes when she's in a good mood, so that was nice. Told her about the contract and she said congratulations but then asked if I'd thought more about the Comfort Systems recruiter who left a voicemail last week. Regional operations director, salary plus bonus, full benefits. She'd pulled up the Glassdoor page and had it open on the iPad.

I said I'd look at it later. She said I always say that. Which is true. The recruiter calls every eight or nine months and I never call back. Karen thinks it's stubbornness. Maybe it is. But I built this business from one van and a credit card and walking away from it for a salary feels like admitting something I'm not ready to admit.

We didn't argue about it. We never really argue about it. She makes her case, I deflect, we move on to dishes. The pork chops were good. I checked the bank balance on my phone while she loaded the dishwasher, which is a habit I should probably stop doing at the dinner table. Numbers looked fine. They usually do when I check them four times a day.

## Entry 2 - 2025-05-29

Had the best day I've had in months and I need to put it somewhere. One of our installs from last year—the dental clinic on France Ave—called because their system was cycling weird. I drove out myself instead of sending Tyler because I remembered the ductwork in that building is unusual, old construction with some modifications from the seventies.

Spent two hours up in the ceiling tracing the problem. Found it. A damper actuator that was wired backwards from the factory, not our install, original equipment. Fixed it with a twenty-dollar part and the office manager looked at me like I'd performed surgery. She said the last company they used would have quoted them a full replacement.

That right there. That's why I do this. Not the money, not the freedom, but the actual work. Knowing the systems, knowing the buildings, solving the puzzle that nobody else caught because nobody else spent two hours in a ceiling when they could've just sold a new unit. I drove home with the windows down even though it was only fifty-five degrees. Karen asked why I was smiling and I said I fixed a damper actuator and she said she had no idea what that meant but she was glad.

**Nudge:** "What would you lose if you weren't the one up in that ceiling?"

**Response:** I don't know. Probably nothing that matters to anyone but me. Corporate guys fix things too, just with more paperwork. Anyway it was a good day, that's all.

## Entry 3 - 2025-06-01

Sunday and I should be relaxing but I spent the morning going over the books again. Karen's brother Dave called yesterday to say his company is doing a round of layoffs and asked if I had any work for him. Electrical background, good with his hands, reliable. The honest answer is I could use another set of hands for the summer rush. The problem is that hiring Dave means I'm either growing the business or I'm doing my brother-in-law a favor, and those two things need different levels of commitment.

If I bring him on and things slow down in October, I'm the one who has to let Karen's brother go. If I don't bring him on, I'm the guy who could've helped family and chose the spreadsheet instead. Karen didn't ask me directly but she didn't have to. The way she handed me the phone said enough.

**Nudge:** "Whose business is it when family's involved?"

## Entry 5 - 2025-06-09

The Comfort Systems recruiter called again. This time Karen answered and talked to the guy for ten minutes before handing me the phone. She'd already told him about my experience, how long I've been running the business, all of it. Like she was selling me. I took the phone into the garage and told the recruiter I'd think about it, same as always.

Karen said afterward that she's not trying to run my life, she just wants me to have a backup plan. A backup plan. Like what I built is something that needs a safety net instead of being the safety net. I've kept this thing running through a pandemic, through supply chain problems, through a two-month stretch where our biggest client went bankrupt and didn't pay. I don't need a backup plan. I need people to stop acting like doing it yourself is the risky option when it's the only option I can actually control.

**Nudge:** "Is she wrong about wanting a net, or is it about who's holding it?"

**Response:** Both, I think. I don't mind having a net. I mind someone else deciding what the net looks like. If I go corporate, the stability is real but it's theirs. They can pull it anytime. What I have now is smaller and harder but it's mine. That's the part she doesn't hear when she talks about benefits and salary bands.
