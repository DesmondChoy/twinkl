- **Core Values:** Hedonism
- **Bio:** Margit is a 49-year-old textile artist based in Gothenburg, Sweden, who splits her time between commissioned installation work for public spaces and teaching two days a week at a folk art school. She lives alone in a rented apartment near the harbor with a small studio space she converted from the spare bedroom. After her divorce eight years ago she took on the teaching to stabilize her income, and she still measures her days partly by how much productive work she managed to get done before allowing herself to stop.

## Entry 1 - 2025-10-24

Finished the second panel for the Kungsbacka commission today. When I stepped back and looked at it hung up on the temporary rail, I actually liked it. Not in the way where you talk yourself into liking something because you spent forty hours on it. I genuinely liked the way the indigo section catches the light from the side window. I stood there for a while just looking.

Then I let myself leave the studio at four instead of pushing through to six. Walked down to the harbor and sat on the bench near the ferry terminal. Bought a cardamom bun from the kiosk. It was still warm. The whole walk home I felt good, and for once I didn't ruin it by calculating whether I'd done enough that day to justify leaving early. I just walked.

## Entry 3 - 2025-11-04

Teaching day. The folk school wanted me to stay for the staff meeting after my afternoon class, so I did. It ran an hour longer than it needed to because people kept going in circles about the spring exhibition. I had opinions but didn't push them because I'm only there two days a week and it's not my place to take over other people's planning.

Someone asked what I'm working on in my own studio and I gave a short answer. I don't like talking about commissioned work with the folk school crowd because it always turns into a conversation about whether commercial work counts as real art. It does, for the record. The Kungsbacka library is going to have my panels on their walls for the next twenty years. But I don't feel like defending that every time.

## Entry 4 - 2025-11-05

Long day but a strangely satisfying one. Woke up early because the light was doing that thing it does in November here, grey but somehow bright at the same time, and I wanted to see how it would fall across the third panel. So I was in the studio by seven, still in my wool socks, coffee going cold on the shelf behind me because I forgot about it.

Worked through until about one without stopping. Not because I was forcing myself but because the work was pulling me along. The warp tension on the new section was exactly right and the shuttle was moving the way I wanted. Those stretches don't happen every day and when they do you just stay in them.

After I stopped I heated up leftover soup and ate it standing at the kitchen counter, which I know isn't a proper lunch but it was warm and salty and I was hungry enough that it tasted better than it had any right to. Then I lay down on the sofa for twenty minutes. Just lay there with the radio on low. Didn't set a timer, didn't think about what I should be doing instead. I was tired enough that my body made the decision for me and I didn't argue with it. The afternoon I spent doing the boring parts, measuring and marking the next section, but even that felt okay because the morning had been so good.

## Entry 5 - 2025-11-05

Writing again because something small happened after I put this down earlier. I was brushing my teeth before bed and noticed I'd left the studio light on, so I went in to switch it off. Ended up standing there in the dark with just the hallway light coming through the door, looking at the panels. They looked different at night. Quieter.

I almost went back to the loom. There was that pull, that voice that says you could get another hour in. But I didn't. I turned the light off and closed the door. It felt like a small right thing, choosing to stop. Going to bed now.

**Nudge:** "What made stopping feel right tonight?"

**Response:** Because the day had already been full. I didn't owe it anything else.
