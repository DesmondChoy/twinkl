# Persona 5b836f2b: Mathilde Dupont

## Profile
- **Persona ID:** 5b836f2b
- **Name:** Mathilde Dupont
- **Age:** 18-24
- **Profession:** Artist
- **Culture:** Western European
- **Core Values:** Security
- **Bio:** Mathilde is a 22-year-old illustrator living in Lyon who supplements freelance commissions with three shifts a week at a frame shop near the Presqu'ile. She keeps a separate savings account she tops up on payday before spending on anything else, and she renewed her apartment lease for a second year rather than move to the cheaper but less predictable flatshare her friend Celine keeps suggesting. She turned down a residency application last spring because the stipend was uncertain and it would have meant giving up the frame shop hours she'd spent a year building.

---

## Entry 0 - 2025-07-03

Celine asked me again today if I'd reconsidered the flatshare. She found a third person, a ceramicist she knows from art school, and said it would bring my rent down by almost three hundred euros a month. I told her I'd think about it, which is what I always say. The truth is I already ran the numbers. Even with the savings, the lease is month-to-month, the building doesn't have proper insurance, and I have no idea if these two people pay on time.

I know she thinks I'm making excuses. She said something like, "You could use that money for supplies," which is true, but supplies don't matter if I can't pay rent in December because someone flaked. I stayed quiet after that and she changed the subject. I keep having this feeling like I'm making the boring choice and everyone can see it, but I also know I'd lie awake every night in that flat wondering when the arrangement would fall apart.

**Nudge:** "What would that place need to have for you to say yes?"

**Response:** A proper lease. All three names on it, with a clause about what happens if someone leaves. Basically the same thing I have now but cheaper. Which I know is the whole reason they don't want to do it that way. So there's my answer, I guess.

But I've been working toward this since January and it's the first time someone picked my work without me having to explain it first.

## Entry 3 - 2025-07-14

Spent the morning at the bank because the online transfer for the savings account wasn't going through. Turns out there's a new verification step they added and I needed to update my phone number in person. So that was an hour and a half of my day gone, sitting in a plastic chair watching a number on a screen count up. But I got it sorted. The auto-transfer is set for the 2nd of every month again and the buffer is back where it should be.

The part that's sitting with me is what the woman at the counter said. She asked what the savings were for and I said it was just a general reserve. She sort of smiled and said, "Most people your age don't bother." She meant it as a compliment, I think. But it made me feel strange, like I'm doing something unusual by not wanting to end up stuck. Like it's weird to have a plan for what happens if the freelance work dries up or if Girard closes the shop.

I don't think it's weird. My parents went through a period when I was fifteen where money was genuinely scary, and I remember what that did to my mother's face for about two years. I just don't want to be in that position. But hearing someone point out that most people my age don't do this — I don't know. It made the whole thing feel heavier than it needs to be.

**Nudge:** "What was it about your mother's face specifically?"

**Response:** I don't know, it's not one thing. She just looked tired all the time in a way that went deeper than tired. Like she was doing calculations in her head constantly. Anyway, the point is the transfer is fixed now. I'll check it next month to make sure it actually went through automatically.

## Entry 5 - 2025-07-18

Had a really good afternoon at the shop. A woman came in to get an old watercolour framed — her grandmother's, she said. We spent maybe twenty minutes going through mat options and she kept asking my opinion like it actually mattered. I ended up suggesting a linen mount with a thin oak frame, which is what I would have done for my own work. She seemed genuinely happy. Girard nodded at me from the back, which from him is basically a standing ovation.

I think the reason the frame shop works for me, beyond the money, is that it's predictable. I know what Tuesdays look like. I know what Girard expects. There's a rhythm to it that makes the freelance days feel less chaotic by comparison. Some of my friends think I should quit and go full-time on illustration, and maybe they're right eventually, but right now having those three shifts is like having a floor under me. Everything else can wobble as long as the floor holds.

**Nudge:** "Is the floor something you want to keep, or something you want to stop needing?"

## Entry 6 - 2025-07-21

Had dinner with Celine and Hugo last night. Hugo just quit his job at the design agency to freelance full-time. No savings, no backup plan, just "I'll figure it out." Everyone at the table was congratulating him like he'd done something brave. Celine looked at me at one point and said, "See? It's possible." As if I don't know it's possible. That's not the question.

The question is what happens when it doesn't work out and there's nothing underneath you. But I didn't say that, because I'd sound like someone's aunt. I just smiled and said good for him. I meant it, mostly. But the whole walk home I was annoyed, not at Hugo specifically, just at this idea that keeping something steady is the same as not trying. I am trying. I'm showing at Moreau in October. I just also happen to have rent covered through March regardless of whether anyone buys anything. That's not fear, that's just not being stupid.

## Entry 7 - 2025-07-23

Wednesday. Frame shop in the morning, then home to work on the third Moreau piece. This one's coming together faster than the other two — the composition sorted itself out in my sketchbook last week and I've mostly been executing. Used the good ink I've been rationing since April. Felt right to use it now rather than save it for something hypothetical.

After dinner I updated my invoice spreadsheet. Two freelance payments came in this week — the book cover for that small press in Marseille, and the editorial spot for the food magazine. Both on time, which doesn't always happen. Logged them, moved the savings portion over, updated the running total. It's not a lot but it's growing. At this rate I'll hit my year-end target by November.

I also renewed my phone contract today instead of switching to the cheaper provider Celine recommended. Her provider has better rates but they lock you into a twelve-month plan with an exit fee, and I've heard mixed things about their coverage outside the city. Mine costs eight euros more per month but I've had it for four years and it just works. Sometimes I think the small extra cost is worth not having to think about it.

**Nudge:** "You're paying for certainty a lot — the lease, the phone, the frame shop. Is that ever too much?"

**Response:** Maybe. I add it up sometimes and think, if I dropped all the safe choices I'd have an extra four or five hundred euros a month. That's materials, that's a short course, that's two months closer to not needing the frame shop at all. But then I imagine actually doing it and my chest gets tight. It's like — I know the maths says take the risk, but my body won't let me. I don't know if that's wisdom or just a thing I haven't worked through yet.
