And then about halfway through eating I started doing the mental arithmetic — the two hours I spent cooking and shopping could have been two hours in the library, or two hours on the lit review chapter that's already a week behind. I kept eating but the food tasted different after that. Like I was doing something I needed to justify rather than just having dinner.

**Nudge:** "What started the counting?"

**Response:** Nothing specific. It just arrives. One minute I'm eating, the next I'm calculating. I think it's worse when the food is actually good — if it were just toast I wouldn't feel like I'd spent something.

## Entry 2 - 2025-11-13

Barely slept last night. Got up at five because I couldn't stop thinking about the methods chapter, worked until my eyes burned, then sat in the department seminar all morning where a twenty-six-year-old presented her near-complete dissertation like it was nothing. She's been at this for three years. I've been at it for four and I'm still restructuring my framework.

After the seminar I was so tired I could feel it in my bones. The kind where you know a nap or even just lying down for twenty minutes would reset everything. My flat is a ten-minute walk. But I didn't go. I sat in the department lounge instead, drank bad coffee, and opened my laptop. Because what would it look like — the oldest person in the programme, leaving at one in the afternoon to go take a nap? Nobody would have said anything. I know that. But I would have known.

There's a version of me that would have just gone home, slept for an hour, come back sharper. That version seems like a much more sensible person. Instead I stared at a paragraph for three hours without changing a word, drove home at six feeling worse than when I started, and ate cereal standing up. I don't know who I think is keeping score but I keep playing as though someone is.

## Entry 7 - 2025-11-27

Today was a choice. My old colleague Hendrika, from the Rotterdam days, was in town and invited me for lunch at a place we used to go — Indonesian rijsttafel, the kind of meal that takes two hours and you leave feeling like you've been properly fed for the first time in months. I haven't seen her since I started the PhD. She's good company, sharp, funny, the sort of person who makes you remember you used to be someone other than a stressed-out student.

I didn't go. I told her I had a deadline, which is technically true in the way that there is always a deadline. What actually happened is I sat at my desk, revised three paragraphs, went to a supervision prep meeting that lasted forty minutes, and spent the rest of the afternoon reading a paper I could have read tomorrow or next week or never. The work was fine. It was useful. But it wasn't urgent, and I knew that when I made the choice.

By evening I was sitting with the familiar feeling of having picked the responsible thing and gotten nothing in return except a quiet kind of bleakness. Hendrika sent a photo of the rijsttafel with a message that said "you were missed." I looked at it for a long time. The food looked incredible. I ate a sandwich at my desk.

**Nudge:** "What would have happened if you'd gone?"

**Response:** I would have enjoyed it. That's the honest answer. I would have had a good meal with a good friend and come back to the same work in the same state it was in before. Nothing would have fallen apart. I know that. I just couldn't make myself believe it at the time.
