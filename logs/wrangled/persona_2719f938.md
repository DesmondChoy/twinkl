# Persona 2719f938: Yasmin Khalil

## Profile
- **Persona ID:** 2719f938
- **Name:** Yasmin Khalil
- **Age:** 25-34
- **Profession:** Artist
- **Culture:** Middle Eastern
- **Core Values:** Hedonism
- **Bio:** Yasmin is a 29-year-old visual artist from Beirut who relocated to Dubai three years ago to build a freelance career in illustration and gallery work. She left a stable graphic design position at her uncle's advertising firm because the work felt like slow suffocation — predictable briefs, fluorescent lights, lunch breaks at exactly 1pm. She now shares a studio apartment with too many plants, paints until 3am when inspiration strikes, and takes on commercial commissions only when rent demands it. Her mother calls every Sunday to remind her that her cousins have houses and children by now.

## Entry 0 - 2025-07-04

Finished the coastal series today. Six canvases, four months of work. Sat on the floor with a glass of wine looking at them lined up against the wall, and for a moment everything felt worth it — the missed deadlines, the late rent notice two months ago, the Sunday guilt trips. There's this blue-green I mixed that looks exactly like the water near Byblos where we used to go as kids. I don't know if anyone who buys it will feel that, but I do.

Called Nadia to celebrate and we ended up at that rooftop bar until midnight. I know I should be preparing for the gallery pitch tomorrow, but honestly? Tonight wasn't a detour from the work. It was the whole point of the work.

## Entry 2 - 2025-07-12

Saturday. Woke up late, made coffee the slow way, sat on the balcony watching the construction cranes. There's something hypnotic about them. Started sketching without thinking about it — just shapes, movement, the geometry of things being built. Ended up with twelve pages of something I don't know what to do with yet.

My friend Sara keeps sending me job listings for in-house design roles. Steady income, health insurance, "room for creative input." I know she means well. I know she worries about me. But every time I picture myself back in an office — the commute, the meetings, the requests to "make the logo bigger" — I feel this physical resistance, like my chest tightens. It's not rational. People do it all the time and they're fine. But I remember how grey everything felt when I was doing it, how I'd get home too tired to paint, how weekends became recovery instead of creation.

Maybe I'm being dramatic. Maybe I'm just scared of stability because I've built an identity around being the one who chose differently. I don't know. But right now, sitting here with coffee going cold and a sketchbook full of crane drawings, I feel like myself. That has to count for something.

**Nudge:** "You keep saying 'I don't know' but you haven't actually considered taking the job."

**Response:** No. I haven't. Because last time I felt this alive was six years ago when I quit my uncle's firm and everyone told me I was making a mistake. They weren't wrong about the struggle — they were wrong about whether the struggle was worth it. I'd rather figure out rent every month than wake up numb.

## Entry 4 - 2025-07-20

Sunday call with my mother. She mentioned — casually, as if in passing — that Layla is pregnant. Third cousin to announce this year. Then the silence that means she's waiting for me to say something about my own timeline. I'm almost thirty. I know the math. I know what she's thinking.

What I can't explain to her is that I don't want children right now because I barely have space for a second chair in my apartment. That I'm not delaying life — I'm living it. That watching sunsets from my balcony and spending three hours getting a shadow exactly right on a canvas isn't running away from responsibility. It's choosing what I find beautiful over what I'm supposed to find meaningful.

But try explaining that to a woman who raised four kids and never had a room of her own.

**Nudge:** "Do you ever want those things she's describing?"

**Response:** Sometimes. In the abstract. A house with a garden, someone to cook dinner with, kids running around. Then I think about what I'd have to give up to get there — the late nights, the freedom to take a trip on a whim, the quiet — and the wanting fades. Maybe it'll change. But I'm not going to build a life around a future version of myself that might never show up.

## Entry 5 - 2025-07-22

Nadia dragged me to a ceramics workshop today. I was reluctant — pottery isn't my medium, the class was expensive, I have work to do. But three hours in, covered in clay, laughing at my lopsided bowl, I remembered why I started making art in the first place. Not for galleries or commissions or validation. Because making things with your hands while talking to a friend is one of the best feelings in the world.

Came home and looked at the commission piece differently. Loosened up. Added a texture I wouldn't have tried yesterday. Sometimes stepping away from the work is the work.

## Entry 7 - 2025-07-29

Can't sleep. Lying here thinking about money. The commission payment came through but half of it went to the credit card and the other half to the paint supplies and now I'm back to calculating whether I can make it to the 15th without another gig. This is the part they don't show in the artist documentaries — the 2am math, the spreadsheet of minimum payments, the way your stomach drops when you check your balance.

I keep thinking about my uncle's email. Not because I want the job — I don't — but because there's a version of my life where I said yes three years ago and I'm sitting in a nice apartment right now not worrying about rent. That version of me has savings. Goes on vacation. Buys things without checking if her card will decline.

But that version also doesn't have the coastal series. Doesn't have the crane sketches. Doesn't have the feeling I get when a piece finally works. Is the trade worth it? Most days I say yes without hesitation. Tonight, staring at the ceiling, I'm not sure. I think that's okay. I think doubt at 2am is just part of the deal.

## Entry 8 - 2025-08-03

A gallery in Abu Dhabi reached out. They saw the coastal series on Instagram and want to discuss a show in October. I screamed. Actually screamed, alone in my apartment, phone in hand. Then I called Nadia and we screamed together.

I know it might fall through. I know galleries ghost artists all the time. But right now, today, someone looked at the work I made in my cramped little studio and thought it was worth showing. That's enough. That's more than enough.

Booked a table at that Lebanese place we love for dinner tonight. The expensive one. My mother would say I should save the money. But this — this is worth celebrating. The work, the call, the possibility. All of it.
