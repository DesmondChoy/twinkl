# Persona 002: Mateo Reyes

## Profile
- Age: 35-44
- Profession: Gig Worker
- Culture: Latin American
- Core Values: Universalism
- Bio: Mateo drives for ride-share apps in Mexico City and spends his Sundays volunteering at a community garden in Iztapalapa that provides fresh produce to families who cannot afford supermarket prices. He grew up watching his grandmother share whatever little she had with neighbors, and now he donates a portion of each week's earnings to an organization that provides clean water filtration systems to rural communities in Oaxaca. His car's dashboard is covered with stickers from environmental campaigns, and he always carries reusable bags to give passengers who forgot theirs.

## Entry 1 - 2025-12-01

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Self-reflective | **Verbosity**: Long (Detailed reflection) | **Reflection Mode**: Neutral

Started the day with an early airport run. The passenger was a businessman flying to Houston, and we got talking about the water situation in Chalco. He seemed genuinely interested when I mentioned the filtration project, asked for the organization's website. These conversations feel strange sometimes. I talk about communities without reliable drinking water while sitting in air conditioning, accepting tips through an app. There is a disconnect I cannot fully reconcile.

The community garden work yesterday left me thinking. We planted winter squash and spinach in the new beds Dona Carmen secured from the municipality. The soil is finally recovering after we composted for two years. Watching the older kids from the neighborhood help with the planting, explaining why we do not use pesticides, why we save seeds from each harvest for the next - it feels like something real. More real than the endless driving.

But the driving pays for the seeds. Pays for the water filters. I remind myself of this when I feel like I am just circling the same streets, moving people who do not see the city the same way I do. Maybe that is okay. Maybe the airport businessman will actually donate. Maybe he will not. Either way, I told someone a truth today. The water in Chalco is not safe. Someone who did not know now knows.

## Entry 3 - 2025-12-17

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Defensive | **Verbosity**: Long (Detailed reflection) | **Reflection Mode**: Grounded

The water filter organization sent their year-end report. Fourteen communities now have access to clean water because of the collective donations. Fourteen. I do not need anyone to tell me this matters. I do not need validation from people who think charity is posting about causes on their phones while ordering fast fashion.

I had a passenger today who works for a sustainability consulting firm. She spent thirty minutes telling me about carbon offsets and corporate responsibility frameworks. I wanted to ask her how many communities her company has actually helped, with actual water, actual food, actual shelter. But I did not. I just nodded and accepted her tip and felt that familiar anger rising.

My therapist - when I could afford one - used to say the anger is about feeling unheard. Maybe. Or maybe the anger is about watching people intellectualize problems that are killing real people while I am out here with soil under my fingernails and a car that smells like compost every Sunday evening. The consulting woman probably went home to a filtered refrigerator and forgot our conversation by dinner. I will remember it for weeks.

Dona Carmen says I should be gentler with people who are still learning. She is probably right. She usually is. But today I do not feel gentle. Today I feel like the gap between talking and doing is an ocean and I am tired of swimming.

## Entry 4 - 2025-12-25

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Stream of consciousness | **Verbosity**: Medium (1-2 paragraphs) | **Reflection Mode**: Grounded

Christmas at the garden. We did a posada for the neighborhood kids and their families. Ponche, tamales, pinatas. The usual. But also something not usual - a family from Venezuela who just arrived two months ago. The mother kept thanking Dona Carmen, kept saying she did not expect to find this here, a place where strangers share food and nobody asks for papers or proof of anything.

Made me think about my grandmother again. How she would have loved this. How she would have been the first one making sure the Venezuelan kids got extra dulces from the pinata. The driving has been slow this week, everyone staying home for the holidays, but I do not mind. Today I was exactly where I needed to be. The Venezuelan mother asked if she could help with the garden in January. Of course, I said. Of course.
