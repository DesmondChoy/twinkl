## Profile
- Age: 35-44
- Profession: Parent (Stay-at-home)
- Culture: Latin American
- Core Values: Benevolence, Tradition
- Bio: Gabriela left her accounting job eight years ago when her twins were born, a decision her mother had also made and one that felt obvious at the time. Told Mamá Lucia I couldn't drive her to the novena tonight because I already promised to take Sofia to her friend's quinceañera practice and somehow that made me the bad guy. She didn't say it outright but she did that thing with her mouth, the little press of the lips, and now I'm sitting here in the parking lot waiting for Sofia feeling like I failed at something. Which is ridiculous because I literally cannot be in two places at once.

Miguel said he could take his mother but she wanted me specifically. I don't know why that matters so much to her or why it matters so much to me that I couldn't do it. Sofia's been looking forward to this for weeks. But there's this knot in my stomach like I picked wrong.

## Entry 2 - 2025-11-01

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Emotional/Venting | **Verbosity**: Long (Detailed reflection) | **Reflection Mode**: Grounded

Día de los Muertos and for the first time since she passed, putting out Abuela's photo on the ofrenda didn't make me cry. The kids helped me arrange the marigolds and Mateo asked why we put out her favorite pan dulce if she can't eat it anymore. I tried to explain that it's not about eating, it's about remembering, about keeping her close. He said 'so it's like how we save her chair at Sunday lunch' and I almost lost it right there because yes, exactly, that's exactly it.

Sofia lit the candles and said a prayer she'd written herself. Nothing fancy, just talking to Abuela like she was in the room. Telling her about school, about her quinceañera dress fitting, about how Mamá still makes the tamales the way she taught us. The twins sat there actually quiet for once, just watching the candles flicker.

Miguel squeezed my hand and didn't say anything which was perfect. Some moments don't need words. I looked at my kids looking at photos of people they barely knew or never met and thought: this is how it keeps going. This is how we don't lose them. Abuela would have been proud of that ofrenda. She would have said the marigolds were a little crooked but she would have been proud.

## Entry 4 - 2025-11-17

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Defensive | **Verbosity**: Medium (1-2 paragraphs) | **Reflection Mode**: Unsettled

My cousin Daniela made a comment at the birthday party about how she 'could never' stay home with her kids, how she'd 'lose her mind.' Said it right to my face with that smile like she was complimenting me for surviving something she considers beneath her. I smiled back and said something about how we all make choices that work for our families.

But I've been replaying it all afternoon. The way she said 'your mind' like maybe I've already lost mine. Eight years I've been doing this. Eight years of invisible labor and schedule-juggling and making sure everyone in this house is fed and dressed and where they need to be and emotionally okay. That's not nothing. That's not losing anything. I chose this. My mother chose this. Her mother chose this. It means something.

## Entry 5 - 2025-11-26

### Initial Entry
**Tone**: Self-reflective | **Verbosity**: Long (Detailed reflection) | **Reflection Mode**: Grounded

Thanksgiving at our house this year because Tía Carmen's hip surgery means she can't host. Twenty-three people in our living room and dining room and spilling into the backyard. I was up at 5am starting the turkey and by 2pm I was convinced something would go wrong—not enough chairs, the gravy too thin, someone would bring up politics.

But then everyone arrived and the noise filled the house in that particular way it does when family gathers. Loud and overlapping and slightly chaotic. Mamá Lucia told stories about her childhood in Guadalajara that I've heard a hundred times but the kids haven't, and watching their faces as she described her father's bakery made me realize I'm the bridge. The one who holds the old stories and passes them down.

Sofia helped me serve without being asked. Mateo actually sat through the whole meal without complaining. The twins made a centerpiece at school with paper feathers that shed everywhere but everyone acted like it was beautiful because that's what you do.

Carmen pulled me aside after pie and said 'your abuela would be so proud of what you've built here.' I don't know why that hit so hard. Maybe because I needed someone to see it. The work of it. The love of it. The holding-it-all-together of it. I went to bed exhausted but the good kind. The kind that means something mattered.
