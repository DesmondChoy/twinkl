The base structure is done. All thirty-four panels, cut and sanded and ready for paint. I stood back today and looked at the skeleton of the thing—this rising wave that will eventually hold video projections, sound, the voices of people I've never met whose lives hang in the balance.

I thought about Katrine's feedback. The hope question. And I realized I was wrong to dismiss it entirely. Not because she's right that audiences need comfort, but because hope isn't the opposite of truth. Fatima in Berlin—she had hope. Impossible, stubborn hope. The families I've interviewed for this project, the ones who lost everything and started over—they have hope too. It's not naive. It's a form of resistance.

So I'm adding something. Not a happy ending, but a thread. A wall of handwritten messages from people who've rebuilt their lives after displacement. Their words, their handwriting. Small acts of continuation.

My mother would say I'm softening. Maybe I am. Maybe softening isn't the same as surrender.

The residency runs until 2027. Two more years of this stability. Two more years of being able to worry about the work instead of worrying about money. I'm grateful in a way I couldn't have articulated ten years ago. Grateful and still restless. Still wanting to make something true.

Outside, the harbor is gray. Winter light. The water rises imperceptibly, day by day. We're all just trying to stay afloat.
